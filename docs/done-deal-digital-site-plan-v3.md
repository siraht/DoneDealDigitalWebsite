# Done Deal Digital — Complete Site Build Plan (v3)

**Purpose of this document:** This is the single source of truth for building the Done Deal Digital website. It includes public-facing copy direction, UX rules, technical constraints, and internal guardrails that should shape what gets built and what does not. When a tactical choice conflicts with conversion clarity, trust, or operational reality, trust conversion clarity, trust, and operational reality first.

**Important:** Sections labeled **Internal** are planning rules, not public copy. They exist to keep the site aligned with how the business actually needs to operate.

---

## 0. Strategic Guardrails

### Primary Objective for v1
Launch a site that converts skeptical trades business owners from cold outreach, referrals, and direct traffic into qualified conversations.

### Secondary Objective
Make the next sales conversation easier by establishing three things fast:
1. **We understand service businesses.**
2. **We know how to make the phone ring.**
3. **We are clear, direct, and not going to waste their time.**

### What the Site Is Not Trying to Do
- It is **not** trying to win design awards.
- It is **not** trying to impress startup founders or generic B2B marketers.
- It is **not** trying to position Done Deal Digital as a full-service brand agency.
- It is **not** trying to sell every service equally hard on day one.
- It is **not** the place to publish empty proof pages, inflated claims, or vague agency language.

### Offer Priority & Sequencing
**Internal**

Until the proof library is stronger, the site should position services in this order:

1. **Website Builds** — clearest, easiest-to-understand front-end offer; strongest entry point from cold outreach.
2. **Local SEO** — strong follow-on offer once site and Google Business Profile basics are in place.
3. **Paid Ads / Lead Generation** — only for businesses with the budget, responsiveness, reviews, and operational readiness to support it.

Do **not** give all three services identical visual weight on the homepage. The homepage should still mention all services, but Web Design should feel like the clearest entry offer.

### Best-Fit Clients
Done Deal Digital is best positioned for:
- Owner-led or owner-involved **local service businesses**
- Trades and adjacent blue-collar service categories
- Companies with roughly **1-20 employees**
- Businesses serving **one local market or a handful of nearby markets**
- Businesses that answer the phone, can follow up fast, and care about lead quality
- Businesses with an outdated site, weak Google presence, poor conversion flow, or wasted ad spend
- Businesses that value clarity, speed, and practical execution over “strategy decks”

### Poor-Fit Clients
The site should quietly discourage:
- E-commerce companies
- Restaurants, bars, and hospitality concepts
- Enterprise or multi-state organizations with layered approval chains
- Businesses looking for a pure social-media-content shop
- Businesses expecting guaranteed rankings, guaranteed lead volume, or miracle results on tiny budgets
- Businesses with no one available to answer the phone or follow up on leads
- Projects that are really custom software builds, portals, marketplaces, or SaaS products

### Claims Discipline
Every public claim must pass one of these tests:
- **Proven:** We can back it up with real work, documented experience, or an actual process we control.
- **Qualified:** It is framed as a target, common range, or best-fit outcome rather than a promise.
- **Omitted:** If it cannot be proven or responsibly qualified, it does not go on the site.

That means:
- No “#1 rankings” language
- No guaranteed lead counts
- No fake urgency
- No fake testimonials, fake client logos, or fake case studies
- No “Google-certified” or partner-style trust signals unless they are literally true and current
- No inflated metrics that sound impressive but do not matter to the prospect

### Launch Decisions That Must Be Finalized Before Code Freeze
**Internal**

These are the biggest remaining business decisions. The site can be built before all of them are publicly disclosed, but they must be operationally settled before launch:

- **Pricing stance:** fully custom only, or “starts at” pricing for web builds
- **Hosting / maintenance model:** included, optional, or separate monthly support plan
- **Geographic scope:** local / regional / nationwide; public copy must state this consistently once chosen
- **Lead response owner:** who receives inbound leads, who follows up, and how quickly
- **Ongoing-service minimums:** especially for SEO and paid ads
- **Client ownership policy:** domain, analytics, GBP, ad accounts, site files, and access
- **Case study publication threshold:** see Section 8

## 1. Brand Identity & Positioning

### Who We Are
Done Deal Digital is a performance-focused web design and digital marketing agency built for blue-collar, service-oriented businesses — plumbers, HVAC contractors, roofers, electricians, landscapers, and similar trades. We build websites, local visibility systems, and paid lead-generation campaigns designed to help service businesses win more calls and better leads.

### Positioning Statement
Done Deal Digital helps local service businesses look credible fast, get found more often, and convert more visitors into calls — without the agency runaround.

### Who We're Talking To
Our prospect is usually the owner, operator, or working manager of a trades business. Public copy should speak to that person directly, but it should also feel credible to the spouse, office manager, or second decision-maker who may look at the site later.

Typical traits:
- Running a crew of **1-15 people**
- Making decisions between jobs, from the truck, or late at night after work
- Skeptical of marketing because he’s been burned before
- Not deeply technical, but very capable and quick to smell nonsense
- Values proof, speed, and straight answers over polish and buzzwords
- Judges credibility by gut feel: “Do these people know what matters to my business, and will they actually follow through?”

### What We Are Not
- Not a trend-chasing creative agency
- Not a “growth hacking” shop
- Not a content studio selling social packages as the main event
- Not a generic brochure-site factory for every industry
- Not trying to sound like a VC-backed SaaS company

### Brand Voice
- **Direct.** Short sentences. No filler. Say exactly what we mean.
- **Concrete.** Use specifics, examples, deliverables, timeframes, and outcomes.
- **Confident, not chest-thumping.** Calm authority beats hype.
- **Trade-fluent, not trade-cosplay.** We can use the occasional trades metaphor, but we should not overdo it.
- **Results-first.** Tie language back to leads, calls, booked jobs, credibility, and revenue.
- **Operator-minded.** The tone should feel like someone who has had to make payroll and watch marketing dollars closely.

### Brand Voice — Guardrails
- Use trades references **sparingly**. A few strong phrases land harder than constant blue-collar cosplay.
- Write to the owner/operator, not to a demographic stereotype.
- Keep the language masculine only when it is natural in conversation; avoid hard-coded gender assumptions in public copy.
- Use plain English for technical concepts: “Google Business Profile” before “entity optimization.”
- Sound like someone good at the work, not someone performing expertise.

### Brand Voice — Don'ts
- Don't use startup jargon: “synergy,” “ecosystem,” “disrupt,” “scale faster”
- Don't use vague agency-speak: “holistic strategy,” “brand storytelling,” “content journey”
- Don't use macho hype copy: “dominate,” “crush the competition,” “print money,” “lead machine” as default language
- Don't be cute, ironic, or self-consciously clever
- Don't over-explain obvious things
- Don't make promises that sound bigger than the business can realistically support

### Messaging Priorities
In order of importance, site messaging should communicate:
1. **Who we help**
2. **What problem we solve**
3. **What the offer includes**
4. **Why we can be trusted**
5. **What happens next**

The tech stack, design philosophy, and internal process all matter, but they should support those five points — not replace them.

## 2. Design System

### Core Philosophy
The design should feel like **a sharp-looking truck wrap crossed with a well-run local service brand** — confident, bold, useful, and built to work. It should look polished enough that business owners trust us with their website, but grounded enough that we still feel like practical operators.

### Visual Goal
The site should not look like:
- a template marketplace demo
- a startup landing page
- a contractor’s own service site
- a dark-mode tech dashboard

It should look like a serious agency for service businesses.

### Layout Principles
- **Grid-based, clean sections.** No overlapping collage layouts, no diagonal cuts, no parallax tricks.
- **Big hierarchy.** Users should understand the page in a fast mobile skim.
- **Strong section rhythm.** Alternate dense information sections with breathing room.
- **One clear primary action at a time.** Buttons should be obvious and easy to hit.
- **Defined edges.** Sharp corners or very subtle radius only. Borders should feel intentional.
- **Desktop restraint.** Keep the layout strong, but do not overcomplicate it just because there is more room.
- **Subtle nav separators only on desktop.** Vertical line separators can work, but keep them understated.

### Design Tokens (Working Values)
Use these as the default visual system unless a stronger, fully-tested alternative is approved:

- **Primary navy:** `#0B1F33`
- **Secondary navy:** `#102A43`
- **Accent orange:** `#F47C20`
- **Body text dark:** `#0F172A`
- **Muted text:** `#475569`
- **Off-white surface:** `#F8FAFC`
- **Pure white:** `#FFFFFF`

**Important contrast rule:** Do **not** use white text on the orange button fill. White-on-orange fails too easily. Use dark navy text on orange buttons, and test every text/background pair for WCAG AA.

### Typography
- **Headings:** Use a condensed face such as **Barlow Condensed** or an equally strong, readable alternative.
- **Body / UI text:** Use a clean, highly readable sans-serif such as **Inter**.
- **Use all-caps selectively.** All-caps can work for short labels, CTA buttons, eyebrow text, and very short headlines. Do **not** force long multi-line hero headlines or dense subheads into all-caps if readability suffers.
- **Body text minimum:** 16px
- **Comfortable line length:** cap long-form body copy around 65-70 characters where practical

### Spacing & Density
- **Max content width:** ~1200px
- **Comfortable reading width for body copy:** ~68ch
- **Mobile side gutters:** ~20-24px
- **Desktop section padding:** generous, but avoid excessive empty space that makes the site feel abstract or premium-for-premium’s-sake
- **Card padding:** enough to feel substantial; avoid cramped “dashboard” cards

### Texture & Atmosphere
- Subtle concrete, asphalt, grid, or blueprint texture is fine
- Use diagonal stripe motifs **sparingly**
- One texture treatment per section is enough
- Do **not** place heavy texture directly under small body text
- The site should feel structured and industrial, not gimmicky

### Color & Surface Strategy
Do not make the entire site dark. A full dark site can feel heavy, fatiguing, and harder to scan.

Recommended surface balance:
- Roughly **60-70% clean light or controlled dark surfaces**
- Roughly **30-40% accent/texture-heavy surfaces**

Use dark sections strategically for:
- hero
- service CTAs
- footer
- major separators

### Imagery Direction
- Use real tradespeople imagery to signal the audience
- Prefer action shots over posed portraits
- Avoid handshake stock, laptop pointing, and suit-and-boardroom imagery
- As soon as possible, shift trust-building away from stock and toward:
  - **real founder photos**
  - **real website screenshots**
  - **real reporting screenshots**
  - **real case-study visuals**

**Important:** The site should not feel like a plumber site. Trade imagery should support the message, but actual work samples and founder presence should carry trust.

### Screenshots & Mockups
When possible:
- Show websites on devices
- Show real before/after site screenshots
- Show small snippets of real work rather than full-page fake mockups
- Caption screenshots clearly so they read as proof, not decoration

### Icons
- Use one icon library consistently
- Simple line or solid icons only
- No 3D icons
- No emoji-style illustration

### Mobile-First Requirements
- **Sticky mobile CTA bar** fixed to the bottom with `CALL NOW` + `GET A QUOTE`
- Respect safe-area insets on iPhone (`env(safe-area-inset-bottom)`)
- Hide or disable the sticky bar when:
  - the contact form is visible
  - the on-screen keyboard is open
  - the thank-you page is shown
- **Tap targets:** minimum 44px height
- **No horizontal scroll.** Test at **320px**, **360px**, **390px**, and **414px**
- No tiny links clustered together in cards or footers
- Content must make sense in 30-second bursts

### Motion & Interaction
- Keep motion subtle
- No auto-rotating carousels
- No decorative scroll animations that delay comprehension
- Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`
- Use interaction to clarify state, not to show off

**Q: What’s included, and what counts as extra?**
A: A standard site includes the agreed page scope, design, build, copy guidance, launch setup, and one structured revision round. Extra pages, complex integrations, custom automations, ecommerce, advanced booking flows, and major scope changes are quoted separately.

**Q: Are there ongoing costs after launch?**
A: There can be. Domain, hosting, maintenance, SEO, and paid ads are not all the same thing. We’ll spell out what is one-time, what is recurring, and what is optional before you commit.

**Q: Do I own my website and accounts?**
A: The goal should always be for the client to own the key assets: domain, analytics, Google Business Profile, and ad accounts wherever possible. If we set something up for you, ownership and access should be clear in writing.

**Q: How many revisions are included?**

**Q: Will I be able to update the site myself?**

A: We can. The key is making the hosting and maintenance model explicit so you know what is included, what is optional, and who is responsible for what.

**Q: Will my site work on mobile?**

## 6. Technical Notes

### v1 Stack
- **Framework:** Astro
- **Component System:** Bookshop
- **Hosting:** Netlify
- **Forms:** Netlify Forms
- **Analytics:** Google Analytics 4
- **Search:** Google Search Console

### CMS Decision
**Critical correction from v2:**
Do **not** keep “Sveltia CMS or CloudCannon” unresolved indefinitely.

Recommended v1 decision:
- Build the site so content is structured and CMS-ready
- Do **not** let CMS selection block launch
- If no-code editing is required at launch, choose **CloudCannon**, because it has native Bookshop support and a stronger visual-editing path
- If no-code editing is **not** required at launch, ship without a CMS and keep content in Astro content collections / data files until it becomes necessary

**Why this is stronger:**
A static agency site with a small number of core pages does not need CMS complexity to justify launch. The v2 plan treats CMS choice like a foundational requirement when it is really a secondary workflow decision.

### Content Architecture
Recommended structure:
- global site settings in a single data file
- page content in Astro content collections or front matter
- FAQ items as data-driven content
- case studies as their own collection
- contact CTA parameters passed via query string
- reusable sections mapped cleanly to Bookshop components

### Performance Requirements
This site sells speed, so speed is part of the brand.

Targets:
- **90+ Lighthouse** on representative pages
- strong **Core Web Vitals** in real-world conditions
- no obvious lag on mid-range mobile devices

Requirements:
- Use Astro’s default low-JS approach
- Hydrate only genuinely interactive elements
- Use Astro image tooling (`<Image />` / `<Picture />`) for local assets
- Serve responsive images
- Lazy-load below-the-fold images
- Avoid heavy embeds above the fold
- Avoid unnecessary third-party widgets
- Subset fonts and use `font-display: swap`
- Inline or otherwise prioritize critical styles

**Internal correction from v2:**
A live Google Maps embed is often too heavy to deserve default inclusion. Use a lighter alternative unless the embed genuinely adds conversion value.

### Forms & Spam Prevention
- Netlify detects forms by parsing **static HTML at deploy time**
- The form cannot be client-rendered only
- Enable automatic form detection in Netlify
- Add a honeypot immediately
- Use a custom success page (`/thank-you`)
- Test form detection after deploy, not just locally

### Analytics & Tracking
Base events to track:
- `page_view`
- `cta_click`
- `phone_click`
- `form_start`
- `generate_lead`

Recommendations:
- mark `generate_lead` as a **key event**
- capture source page and service intent
- capture campaign parameters when available
- keep naming consistent and simple
- avoid event sprawl

**Internal note:**
Do not add Google Tag Manager by default unless it becomes necessary for ad pixels or more complex tag orchestration. For the v1 site, direct GA4 tagging may be simpler and lighter.

### SEO Baseline
Even if cold outreach remains the main acquisition channel, the site must still be technically solid.

Requirements:
- unique title tags and meta descriptions
- one clear H1 per page
- canonical URLs
- sitemap
- robots.txt

- **Blog / resource section** — only if there is a real distribution plan; do not create a blog graveyard
- **A/B testing** — only once traffic supports meaningful conclusions
- **Referral program** — strong fit once satisfied clients exist
- **Live chat** — only if someone can actually respond
- **Client portal** — far later, only if it solves a real operational problem
